The Only Story

At dinnertime my wife often says to our two girls, “Tell us about your day.” And the response: “Everything, jelly, kickball, gum, inky tongue, stinky Band Aids, is sexy a bad word, there’s more—” There’s often some very good stuff in there, but you’ve got to listen long and concentrate. I mean really concentrate.

So one day I had an idea. Simple really. “Tell us one thing about your day. Not two. Not ten. Only one.”

Only one?

Their minds went busy—sifting the day for gold. And then one of them spoke: hugging a friend at recess because both were sad about time outs. And then the next daughter said hers: a worm cracking on a sunny path, carrying the dried-out body to a puddle. And the family was engrossed. We tried Only One again the next night. And a week later. And here’s what I learned: limit people to one thing and you get their most important thing. And this is what happens next: everyone wants to listen.

And this got me thinking. The grownup world is much like the kid world. Just our babble comes in the form of emails, text messages and Facebook posts. Coworkers and advertisers, old high school friends and celebrities, all saying, Listen to me, this is important. And how do people get heard above the noise? They use exclamation points! They post to Twitter four times a day. They all send more. And yet, how I crave a world that is simple. A world where priorities are clear and inboxes empty.

What would happen, I wondered, if grownups could say only one thing? Would they say only what mattered?

I told a few friends I had a big idea. A messaging service where you can send only one message per month.

They responded, “That’s preposterous” and “You’re kidding, right?” and “You realize that with Gmail and Facebook I get unlimited messages. And I can text too.”

I said, “That’s my point. Those are junk delivery systems. Do you really want to say, I’ve always loved you with a text? Do you want to send a condolence with email? But if you could send only one message, using your Only One would be your personal stamp of meaning.”

My friend looked away from my eyes, and asked, “How are the girls?”

Yet, I thought, they are wrong. They don’t understand because they have never played the Only One dinnertime game with their kids.

The next day I sketched out the app. A button here, a stamp there. And I persuaded myself, this isn’t stupid at all. I’m not the only one who wants simplicity. Clarity. Meaning! And so I hired some people to make my sketches real.

And now that the app is done, I wonder, in this world of unlimited free messaging, is Only One the dumbest idea ever? Or is it actually a brilliant solution to the problem of inky-tongue-stinky-band-aids? I honestly do not know, and suspect it is both.

And so we shall find out.
